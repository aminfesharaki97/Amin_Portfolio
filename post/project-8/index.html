Summary

The objective of this expansion is to be able to process an additional 165,000 BPSD and successfully separate them into a number of products. In order to do so, the crude distillation unit had to meet specific design requirements. For vessel sizing, the crude column, jet fuel sidestripper, kerosene sidestripper, diesel sidestripper, and ago sidestripper were taken into account and calculated the respective dimensions including diameter and total tangent length based on the column specifications. Moreover, pump calculations were made to find the destination and suction pressure for each pump and control valve design according to the project specification. In addition, a series of heat exchanger networks were modeled to cool down the hot product streams to 100 F. Preheat 1,2, and 3 were used to cool kerosene, diesel, and atmospheric residence product streams. The remaining product streams were cooled to 100 F using water/air utilities. Each network of heat exchangers that was incorporated is then further analysis to include the preliminary mechanical details. Lastly, an estimated cost analysis for the total fixed capital cost is also included and found to be $1,537,500,000 which includes the ISBL capital cost, engineering costs, and contingency. Specific design information can be found under the Recommended Design section for each equipment.

When designing the crude distillation unit, several health and safety and environmental factors were taken into consideration. Gaseous emissions, stormwater and wastewater disposal, and noise pollution must be monitored and recorded appropriately according to federal and state laws as well as local ordinances. Lastly, proper training must be provided to all employees.

Introduction

The purpose of this project is to upgrade an existing crude distillation unit in Irvine, CA from processing 100,000 BPSD of raw crude to 265,000 BPSD. The client, E&C Refining Company, requests that a new unit be built to process the additional 165,000 BPSD as the existing unit is already operating at maximum capacity. This new unit will separate crude oil into a number of products including naphtha, diesel, kerosene, jet fuel, gas oil, offgas, and atmospheric residue. Of these products, diesel, kerosene, jet fuel, and gas oil are fed into stripping towers, where light components are vaporized and removed. These products are then sent to market or to other units to be further processed into saleable material.

Previous work

An oil refinery refines crude oil into petroleum products. Additionally, the crude unit in the refinery is used to separate crude oil into several products, and which are then can be marketed directly to additional processing units for conversion to saleable products. Currently, the E&C Refining Company processes a maximum capacity of 100,000 BPSD of raw crude oil. The purpose of the refinery is to separate the crude oil into different products: offgas, naphtha, jet, kerosene, diesel, gas oil, and atm residue. Due to Irvine’s increasing demand, the E&C Refining Company are expanding

their operational capacity to process a total of 265,000 BPSD of raw oil. To keep up with the increasing demand, an additional crude unit along with new units and a revamp of existing units must be installed or modified to process the remaining 165,000 BPSD. The E&C Refining Upgrade Project will allow the new crude distillation unit will be able to effectively process the 165,000 BPSD mixture of the two crude oils along with the current crude unit processing 100,000 BPSD to give a total of 265,000 BPSD to satisfy Irvine’s oil demand.

Discussion

Pump Discussion

The pumps and control valves are designed in accordance with specified pressure losses across the various equipment and lines surrounding each pump. The accurate design and sizing of these pumps is essential in providing required final pressures to prevent issues such as overpressure and external fire. Assumptions in the calculations include an equal discharge pressure across the rated, minimum, and normal cases. In reality, this pressure varies across the three cases according to the pump’s specific pump curve. However, these pumps are new and therefore do not possess pump curves. Possible sources of error come from errors in column and vessel sizing, as the static head calculation relies on the columns and vessels that the pumps operate on. Nevertheless, these pump designs provide enough accuracy for analysis of the unit at this stage in the project. The final proposed design includes available NPSH, differential pressure, and hydraulic horsepower calculations, which vary among the pumps.

Heat Exchanger Discussion

Our heat exchanger design process began with a simulation of our crude distillation operation, as seen in the PFD section of this report; a raw crude stream was passed through the shell side of three simple heat exchanger units with defined exit temperatures for each unit. These heat exchangers are meant to preheat the crude oil using the significantly hotter product streams, from the distillation tower and various side stripping columns, on the tube side as it is fed to the distillation column.

The goal of our heat exchanger design was to efficiently preheat the incoming crude stream while cooling product streams to 100 for further processing and storage. The use of product streams for preheat service of the crude feed stream, as opposed to fired heaters fueled by natural gas, makes the overall operation more economically viable by reducing the net utilities otherwise required to preheat or cool desired streams. To optimize this concept, we chose our hottest product streams, kerosene, diesel fuel, and atmospheric residuals, to preheat the feed stream. This allows for the feed to preheat quickly for ideal separation in the distillation column while lowering the hottest product streams to temperatures easily lowered by utilities, such as cooling water.

After running our process simulation with PRO/II software, we were able to utilize the output data, seen in the figure below, of our simple heat exchanger models to calculate an efficient heat exchanger configuration for each of the three preheat nodes in our model. Combined with TEMA standards, we then determined the optimal materials specifications to fabricate our exchanger units and the required utilities to cool all the product streams to the desired 100. This process involves integrating the hottest streams over the preheat feed on the basis of temperature as a function of heat duty, graphically, as seen in the figure below, and determining the optimal pairing of hot product streams to each preheat exchanger node. An in-depth example of the design approach can be found in the Appendix section of this report.

D. *Pump Calculations
*To find the destination and suction pressures for each pump, simulation was used to determine to pressure of the destination tray. To take the turbosene pumparound pump as an example, specifications are given as the draw coming out of theoretical tray 6 and entering the column again at theoretical tray 7. According to the text report, the pressure of tray 7 is 7.34 psig and the pressure of tray 6 is 7.24 psig, which serve as the destination and suction pressures, respectively.

Theoretical trays 6 and 7 translate to actual trays 8 and 9. The allowable destination static head is calculated by considering the height of tray 9 from grade, which is calculated as
$Destination\ head = skirt\ height + column\ height + (total\ number\ of\ trays - destination\ tray)(tray\ spacing)$
$Destination\ head = 20\ ft + 11.81\ ft + (39 - 9)(2\ ft) = 91.81\ ft$
Converting this value to psi,
$Destination\ head = 91.81\ ft\ \frac{psi}{2.31\ ft}0.74 = 30.33\ psi$for the normal case
To calculate the rated and minimum cases, the result is simply multiplied by the flow rate percentage squared.

Control valve sizing follows four criteria: minimum of 15 psi at normal flow, 10 psi at rate flow, 50% variable losses at normal flow, and 25% of variable losses at rated flow. Following these criteria, a minimum of 25% of variable losses at rated flow was chosen, which is equivalent to 7.2 psi. Adding all of the pressures together results in the discharge pressure, which is assumed to be equal across all three cases. In this way, the control valve sizing for the normal and minimum cases can be back calculated.

Suction static head is found similar to the discharge static head, this time taking into account the draw at theoretical tray 6 and actual tray 8. Similarly, the calculation results in a pressure of 29.09 psi. Adding these pressures together results in the suction pressure.

The differential pressure can be calculated as the difference between the discharge and suction pressures:
$Differential\ pressure\ = 87.02\ psi - 34.33\ psi = 52.69\ psi = 164.5\ ft$

NPSH is calculated as
$NPSH = Source\ pressure - Vapor\ pressure - Suction\ line\ losses + Elevation\ liquid\ center$

$= 10.79\ ft$
The safety margin is taken as the greater of 3 ft or 10% of the NPSH calculation and is subtracted from the NPSH calculation. Therefore, the allowable NPSH is
$NPSH\ available = 10.79\ ft - 3\ ft = 7.79\ ft$
